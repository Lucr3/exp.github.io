How This Data Was Used

We took global datasets and ran them through Python to make sure everything was accurate and consistent across all the interactive visualizations you see here.

Methodology

Data Cleaning

What We Did

We kept things real. We started with massive global datasets and simply pulled out just the Yemen rows—nothing more. Then we stopped. No tweaking the numbers, no filling in blanks with guesses. If you see gaps in the charts? That's actual missing data from the source. We didn't hide it.

Data Processing

How We Went From Numbers to Charts

Everything you're seeing happens live in your browser. The code grabs the data files and uses D3.js to turn raw numbers into things you can actually see and understand:

  • GPS coordinates turn into dots and regions on the Yemen map.
  • Dates and numbers become lines, spirals, or colored bars that tell the story of this crisis visually.

Limitations

What You Should Know Before Reading the Data

Getting reliable data from a war zone is incredibly hard. Before you dive into the visualizations, remember these things:

  • Missing data: Some years have zero data—especially around education—because the systems simply weren't there anymore.
  • Isolated zones: The most remote, unreachable areas? They might not show up in conflict reports at all.
  • Perspective: Google Trends only tells us what people type into a search box. It says nothing about what's truly happening on the ground.
